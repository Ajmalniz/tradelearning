What Is Sim Trading?

Sim trading (also called paper trading) means practicing the strategy with fake money on real charts with real market data. You see real price movement. You draw real trend lines. You wait for real breaks. You place real orders — but with simulated account funds.

"The testing aspect — remember, we've got our upward trend line, we've got our downward trend line. We understand that when price breaks the trend line, it's our action. Now we get the reps in on the charts so we can see that it works and build that muscle memory."
🧠

Why Muscle Memory Matters

Reading about how to draw a trend line is different from doing it 50 times on live charts. Sim trading builds the automatic, instinctive knowledge that comes from repetition. After enough reps, you stop thinking about the rules — you just see them on the chart.

TradingView Sim Mode

TradingView has a built-in paper trading mode that lets you simulate trades directly on charts:

  • Go to the bottom of your TradingView chart screen
  • Look for the Trading Panel option
  • Select paper trading — it uses fake money (you'll see an account balance, typically $100,000 by default)
  • Place orders exactly as you would with real money — buy, sell, set stop-losses
  • Watch the trade play out on the live chart
💡

Use the Most Realistic Number

When starting sim trading, set your paper account balance to match what you'll actually be trading with in real life. If you plan to trade with $5,000, set the sim account to $5,000. This keeps your fee calculations and position sizing realistic — so the habits you build match your real trading situation.

Your Sim Trading Session — Step by Step

  1. 1

    Open a chart and select a time frame to trade on

    Start with crude oil or any instrument you're interested in. Go to the 5-minute chart for your final trading time frame.

  2. 2

    Do your full top-down analysis

    Start at monthly → work down. Draw all your trend lines at every time frame. Connect them all. Arrive at your 5-minute chart with a complete picture.

  3. 3

    Come in with steeper lines on the 5-minute

    At the 5-minute chart, draw additional trend lines that capture the most recent price action. These should be steeper and more precise than your higher time frame lines.

  4. 4

    Wait for price to approach a line

    Watch. Be patient. Don't force a trade. Price will move toward one of your lines. Your job is simply to wait.

  5. 5

    Identify the break and assess risk

    When price breaks a line, note which one is action and which is safety. Is this a low-risk or high-risk setup? How far is price from the safety line?

  6. 6

    Open the trading panel and place your order

    Open a long or short position based on which line broke. Drag the stop-loss to the other side of the safety line. The panel will show you how much money you'd lose if the stop-loss gets hit.

  7. 7

    Watch and wait

    Stay in the trade. Watch the safety line. If price respects it, stay in. The moment it breaks — close your position.

Getting Your Reps In

The goal of sim trading isn't to make money — it's to get comfortable with the process. Do this session repeatedly until it becomes automatic: draw lines → wait → act → manage → exit. Each rep builds the neural pathways that make you faster, calmer, and more decisive when real money is on the line.

🔁

Repetition Is the Teacher

Do the top-down analysis every session. Even if you did it yesterday — do it again. Markets move. Lines need updating. The process of doing it daily is what makes you fluent. After 30 sim sessions, you'll draw trend lines without thinking about the rules.
