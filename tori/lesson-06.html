The 1-2% Rule —
Stop-Loss Placement & Position Sizing

Your fee, your fire extinguisher, and why the stop-loss must always go on the OTHER side of the safety line.

⏱ 12 min🟡 Intermediate💰 Risk Management

What Is Your "Fee"?

In Tori's system, your maximum acceptable loss per trade is called your fee. Think of it like a cost of doing business — the price you're willing to pay if this particular trade doesn't work out.

"Fee equals loss. Your fee should be 1 to 2% of your capital."

This is the industry-wide rule of thumb. It's not arbitrary — it's built on probability theory. If you risk only 2% per trade, you would need to lose 50 consecutive trades to wipe out your account. The realistic chance of that is extremely slim.

The Formula

Fee = 1% to 2% of Capital
Your maximum loss per trade should never exceed 2% of your total trading capital

All Relative to Capital Size

A $60 loss on a $3,000 account is exactly the same risk level as a $2,000 loss on a $100,000 account. The number looks very different — but the percentage is identical. Always think in percentages, not dollar amounts.

The Stop-Loss — Your Fire Extinguisher

The stop-loss is a technical order you place that automatically closes your trade if price reaches a certain level. It's your fail-safe.

🧯

Stop-Loss = Fire Extinguisher

When you're making dinner and something goes wrong, what do you have handy? A fire extinguisher. The stop-loss is exactly that. It closes your trade automatically if you get distracted, if you decide not to follow your rules, or if something unexpected happens. Without it, a small mistake can become a catastrophic loss.

WHERE to Place the Stop-Loss

This is critical — and it's one of the most commonly misunderstood parts of risk management. The stop-loss placement rule is simple but non-negotiable:

The stop-loss must always be placed on the OTHER SIDE of the safety line — NOT on the safety line itself.

Why? Because you anticipate that price will respect the safety line and continue moving in your favor. If you put the stop-loss on the safety line, any normal touch of the line would knock you out of your trade prematurely.

Stop-Loss Placement — Where Things Should Go

Action Line
↘ BROKEN (your entry)
Current Price
→ Your position is here
🛡️ Safety Line — price should respect this
🚫 Stop-Loss goes HERE — on the other side
✅

Give the Safety Line Breathing Room

Price often tests (touches) the safety line before continuing in your trade direction. If your stop-loss is right ON the safety line, you'll get stopped out by a normal price movement. Give the line breathing room — place the stop-loss just beyond it, on the other side.

Building on Experience

Tori has traded for 10 years and now sometimes risks 4-7% on a trade based on years of experience, intuition, and the weight of a specific setup. But she is clear: beginners should stick to 1-2%. The discipline of keeping risk small is what allows you to survive long enough to develop that experience in the first place.

⚡

Why This Rule Works

Working with probabilities means keeping individual risk so small that a losing streak can't wipe you out. At 2% per trade, you'd need 50 consecutive losses to blow your account — and the likelihood of 50 correct-analysis trades all going wrong is extremely low if you're following the system.

⚡ Key Takeaways from Lesson 6

  • $3,000 account → max $60 fee. $5,000 → max $100. Always calculate before trading.

  • Stop-loss placement: ALWAYS on the other side of the safety line, NOT on it.
  • Never put the stop-loss on the safety line itself — price needs room to breathe.
  • Think in percentages, not dollars. Risk is always relative to account size.
  • Beginners: stick to 1-2%. Experience lets you adjust later.
