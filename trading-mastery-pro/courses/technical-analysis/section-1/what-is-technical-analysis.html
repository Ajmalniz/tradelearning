Section 1 · Course Introduction

What is Technical Analysis

Technical analysis is the study of price action on charts. Rather than analyzing a company's earnings or a country's economic data, technical analysts focus purely on what price has done and use that to predict what it might do next.

Think of it this way: every time someone buys or sells in a market, that transaction is recorded as a price movement. When millions of trades happen, patterns emerge — and those patterns reflect the collective emotions of all market participants.

A chart is simply a visual record of all past trades. By reading it, you can see where buyers were strong, where sellers took control, and where price might reverse or continue.

Key Terms

Technical Analysis — The study of past price movements on charts to forecast future price direction.
Price Action — The movement of an asset's price over time, plotted on a chart.
Candlestick — A chart element showing the open, high, low, and close price for a given time period.
Timeframe — The duration each candlestick represents (e.g., M15 = 15 minutes, H4 = 4 hours, D1 = 1 day).
📊 Practical Example

On a EURUSD H4 chart, you notice price has bounced three times from the 1.0800 level. Each time it touches 1.0800, buyers step in and push price back up. A technical analyst would mark this as a support level and watch for a potential long trade setup on the next visit to that price.

The foundational premise of technical analysis is that price discounts all information. Everything known about an asset — earnings, news, geopolitical events, insider knowledge — is already reflected in the current price. What matters is what the chart tells us, not what we think we know about fundamentals.

Markets are driven by supply (sellers) and demand (buyers). When demand exceeds supply, price rises. When supply exceeds demand, price falls. Technical analysis is the science of identifying where supply and demand imbalances exist, and trading in the direction of the stronger force.

Price action repeats because human behavior repeats. Fear and greed have been the two dominant market emotions for hundreds of years, producing the same recognizable chart patterns across all markets and all timeframes.

Key Terms

Price Discounts All — The principle that all available information is already reflected in market prices.
Supply & Demand — The balance of sellers vs buyers that determines price direction.
Higher Timeframe (HTF) — A longer chart period (e.g., Daily, Weekly) used to establish the primary market bias.
📊 Practical Example

Imagine a major central bank announces an interest rate hike. Before the announcement, institutional traders have already positioned themselves. By the time retail traders react to the news, the move is often already priced in — sometimes causing price to move in the opposite direction of what you'd expect. This is why chart reading often beats news trading.

💡 Pro Tip

Always start your analysis on the Daily or Weekly chart before drilling down to lower timeframes. The higher timeframe gives you the dominant trend — you then use lower timeframes to time your entry with precision.

At the professional level, technical analysis is understood as a probabilistic framework — not a prediction system. No setup is 100% certain. What a skilled trader does is identify scenarios where the probability of a move in a given direction is significantly higher than the alternative, and then structure a trade with favorable risk-to-reward to exploit that edge.

The key concept is expected value (EV). A strategy with a 40% win rate can be highly profitable if the average winner is 3× the average loser. The math: (0.4 × 3R) − (0.6 × 1R) = 0.6R positive EV per trade. This is why professional traders focus obsessively on their risk parameters rather than their win rate.

Understanding market microstructure elevates TA further: large institutions cannot enter or exit full positions in a single order without moving the market against themselves. They must accumulate or distribute over time, leaving footprints in the form of order blocks, fair value gaps, and liquidity sweeps — all concepts we'll cover in detail in later sections.

Key Terms

Expected Value (EV) — The average profit per trade calculated as (Win Rate × Avg Win) − (Loss Rate × Avg Loss).
Market Microstructure — The mechanics of how orders are processed and how large institutions enter/exit positions.
Edge — A statistical advantage: when executed consistently over many trades, your system produces positive EV.
📊 Practical Example

A professional analyst reviewing XAUUSD daily chart identifies a clean demand zone at $2,280–$2,290. They know that price has swept a liquidity pool below $2,270 twice before reversing. Rather than predicting a rally with certainty, they plan: "If price revisits $2,285 with a bullish H1 confirmation candle, I risk 1% to target $2,340 — a 1:3 R:R setup. Even if this trade type only wins 45% of the time, EV is strongly positive."

💡 Pro Tip

Track every trade in a journal and review your statistics monthly. Your actual win rate and R:R data will tell you whether your edge is real — or imagined. Most retail traders think they have an edge but have never measured it.

Key Takeaway

Technical analysis gives you a structured, repeatable framework to read market sentiment and make decisions based on price behavior rather than news or gut feel. At its core, it's about identifying where probability favors one direction over another.
