Section 3 · Supply & Demand

Integrating S&D with Market Structure

This is the most important rule in supply and demand trading:

  • Only trade DEMAND zones when market structure is BULLISH
  • Only trade SUPPLY zones when market structure is BEARISH

If you trade a demand zone inside a bearish market, you are fighting the trend. The probability is against you, even if the zone looks textbook-perfect. Structure is the filter — use it to eliminate low-probability setups before you even look at zones.

The 3-Step Application

  1. Step 1 — Identify structure on H4 or Daily: is the market making Higher Highs / Higher Lows (bullish) or Lower Highs / Lower Lows (bearish)?
  2. Step 2 — Mark all relevant supply and demand zones on H4
  3. Step 3 — Only trade zones that align with the structure identified in Step 1

Key Terms

Confluence — When multiple independent signals agree, increasing the probability of the expected outcome.
Bias — Your directional expectation for the market based on structure; either bullish or bearish.
With-the-Trend — Trading in the direction of the dominant structure; the highest-probability approach.
📊 Practical Example

Daily XAUUSD is in bullish structure (Higher Highs and Higher Lows clearly visible). On H4, price pulls back to a demand zone at $2,290. This is a valid long setup — structure and zone agree. Now imagine Daily XAUUSD was instead making Lower Lows and Lower Highs (bearish). That same $2,290 demand zone would be ignored — the bias is short, and demand zones in a bearish market are fighting the flow.

💡 Pro Tip

When you first look at a chart, ask one question before anything else: "Is this market bullish or bearish right now?" Write it down. That single word — bullish or bearish — determines which half of all zones on your chart you are allowed to trade. This discipline alone will eliminate the majority of losing trades.

The intermediate approach uses multi-timeframe confluence to layer evidence and filter for only the highest-probability setups.

The Three-Timeframe Framework

  • D1 (Daily) — Sets the bias: bullish or bearish. This is your non-negotiable filter.
  • H4 (4-Hour) — Identifies the significant supply and demand zones. This is your trading zone.
  • H1 (1-Hour) — Provides the entry trigger: a reversal candle, lower-timeframe BOS, or order block retest inside the H4 zone.

When all three align — D1 bullish, H4 demand zone reached, H1 bullish confirmation — you have a high-probability setup. Do not take an H4 zone trade without the H1 confirmation. Do not take the H1 confirmation without the D1 bias.

Confluent Zones

A zone's strength multiplies when it appears on multiple timeframes simultaneously. A D1 demand zone that also contains an H4 demand zone within it is a confluent zone — institutional interest is layered at that level. These zones produce the most explosive reactions and are the highest-probability entries available.

Zone Age and Significance

Zones have a shelf life. If a demand zone on H4 has existed through more than 5 swing periods without being tested, institutional orders at that level may have been cancelled, filled at different prices, or re-positioned. Prioritise zones formed in the last 10–20 candles over zones from weeks ago. Fresh and near = relevant. Old and far = potentially expired.

📊 Practical Example

GBPUSD: D1 bullish structure confirmed. On H4, a fresh demand zone sits at 1.2640–1.2655 — formed just 3 days ago after a BOS. H4 is now pulling back toward that zone. Price reaches 1.2645. On H1, a bullish engulfing candle forms. All three frames agree: D1 bullish + H4 fresh demand zone + H1 bullish trigger = execute the long. Stop below 1.2635. Target next H4 swing high at 1.2760. R:R = 1:11.

💡 Pro Tip

Always work top-down — start at Daily, move to H4, end at H1. Never start at H1 and work upward — you will cherry-pick entry signals that contradict the higher-timeframe reality. The higher timeframe always dominates the lower timeframe.

The expert integration of S&D with market structure is what advanced traders call layered analysis — a systematic framework that produces a binary decision: trade or no trade, with zero ambiguity.

The Full Layered Analysis Framework

  1. HTF (Weekly/Daily) — Macro bias. Is price in a bullish or bearish macro environment? What is the nearest HTF supply or demand zone?
  2. MTF (H4) — Trading zone. What specific S&D zone is price approaching? Is it fresh? Is it within HTF structure?
  3. LTF (H1/M15) — Entry trigger. What is the lowest-timeframe confirmation confirming institutional activity at the MTF zone? (H1 BOS, M15 order block retest, bullish/bearish engulfing candle)

The classic 4-condition entry checklist:

  1. HTF is bullish (or bearish for shorts)
  2. Price is in an HTF demand zone (or supply zone)
  3. H4 shows internal bullish structure — H4 is now making HH/HL within the HTF zone
  4. M15 shows the entry candle: engulfing, pin bar, or order block retest

When HTF and MTF Disagree

The most common mistake: trading an H4 demand zone when the D1 is bearish. What happens? The H4 zone may produce a bounce, but it will be short-lived because the D1 sell orders are much larger than the H4 buy orders. The zone reacts, you get into profit, then price reverses hard against you.

The rule: if HTF and MTF disagree — reduce position size by 50–75% or skip entirely. Track countertrend setups separately in your journal. You will quickly see that the win rate and average R:R are both meaningfully lower than aligned setups.

Structure-Zone Convergence Mapping

Advanced traders maintain a "zone map" — a visual reference of the 3–5 most significant S&D zones across D1 and H4, each labelled with: timeframe, freshness (first / second test), aligned or countertrend, and the required entry trigger. Price always moves between these zones. Instead of reacting to price, you wait at the zone with a pre-defined plan.

Advanced Concepts

Layered Analysis — Systematically confirming a setup across HTF, MTF, and LTF before executing, ensuring all timeframes agree.
Countertrend Setup — Trading a zone that opposes the HTF structure; lower probability, requires reduced size.
Zone Map — A pre-planned reference of key S&D zones with entry criteria defined in advance, eliminating in-the-moment decision errors.
📊 Practical Example

XAUUSD layered analysis: Weekly — bullish (HH/HL, price above all major swing lows). Daily — bullish, approaching D1 demand zone at $2,280–$2,300. H4 — internal structure has turned bullish within the D1 demand zone (H4 made a higher high after a CHoCH). M15 — bullish order block at $2,289 (last bearish M15 candle before the H4 CHoCH). All four layers agree. Entry: $2,289–$2,291. Stop: $2,278 (below D1 demand zone body low). Target: D1 supply zone at $2,360. R:R = 1:6.5. This is a textbook layered analysis trade.

💡 Pro Tip

Track your win rate separately for aligned setups (all timeframes agree) versus countertrend setups (HTF and MTF disagree). Most traders who do this discover that aligned setups have a 60–70% win rate while countertrend setups sit at 35–45%. The data will convince you, more powerfully than any rule, to wait for alignment. Build the habit of recording this in your trade journal from day one.

Key Takeaway

Supply and demand zones are only meaningful in the context of market structure. Trade demand in bullish structure, supply in bearish structure. When all three timeframes agree — macro bias, zone location, and entry trigger — you have a genuinely high-probability setup worth risking capital on.
