Section 3 · Supply & Demand

Supply Zones Explained

A supply zone is an area on the chart where a strong down-move originated. It represents a price level where sellers overwhelmed buyers, creating an imbalance. When price returns to a supply zone, sellers are expected to step in again.

Supply zones are found by looking for two things:

  1. A consolidation or base — a few candles moving sideways or slightly up
  2. Followed by a strong impulsive move DOWN — several large bearish candles dropping sharply

The base area — the consolidation before the drop — IS the supply zone. Mark it as a rectangle on your chart.

Key Terms

Supply Zone — A price area where sellers previously overwhelmed buyers, causing a sharp downward move.
Imbalance — A mismatch between supply and demand that causes rapid price movement in one direction.
Origin of Move — The price area from which an impulsive move began; this is where the zone is drawn.
Impulsive Move — A fast, large-candle move with little or no pullback, signalling strong directional conviction.
📊 Practical Example

On XAUUSD H4, price consolidates between $2,340–$2,350 for 3 candles, then drops sharply to $2,280 within a few hours. The $2,340–$2,350 area is marked as a supply zone. When price later rallies back up to that zone, traders look for short (sell) setups — anticipating that sellers will return to push price down again.

💡 Pro Tip

When drawing a supply zone, use the candle bodies — not the wicks — to define the rectangle. The body represents where price actually traded, while the wick is noise. A cleaner zone boundary leads to more precise entries.

Supply zones represent areas of unfilled institutional sell orders. Large banks and hedge funds cannot place their entire position in a single order — doing so would move the market against them. Instead, they distribute their sell orders across a price range, creating the consolidation you see before the drop.

When price returns to that consolidation area, the institution's remaining unfilled sell orders are still waiting. This is why supply zones often produce reactions on the second and even third visit — each visit fills more orders until the zone is depleted.

Characteristics of a High-Quality Supply Zone

  1. Strong, impulsive departure — multiple large bearish candles with minimal pullback following the consolidation
  2. Fresh zone — never previously tested; each visit consumes orders and reduces the zone's power
  3. Located within bearish market structure — trading supply within a bearish trend aligns probability in your favour
  4. Higher timeframe confluence — a supply zone visible on D1 is more significant than one visible only on H1
📊 Practical Example

GBPUSD on D1 is in bearish structure (Lower Highs, Lower Lows). On H4, you identify a supply zone at 1.2680–1.2700 — a 3-candle consolidation before a 150-pip drop. Price has not revisited that zone. When price rallies back to 1.2680, you have: bearish D1 structure + fresh H4 supply zone = high-probability short setup.

Zone Invalidation Rules

Broken Supply Zone — When price closes ABOVE the supply zone's high with a full-bodied candle, the zone is invalidated. Re-mark it as a potential demand area.
Consumed Zone — A zone that has been tested multiple times loses its potency. Fresh = first test. Stale = 3rd+ test.
💡 Pro Tip

A supply zone is "broken" when price closes above it with a full-bodied candle — not just a wick. Once broken, delete the supply zone label and consider re-marking that area as demand. Levels that flip from supply to demand (or vice versa) are called breaker blocks, covered in Lesson 3.6.

At the advanced level, supply zone analysis incorporates internal zone structure, Fair Value Gaps, and Optimal Trade Entry (OTE) to pinpoint high-probability entries within the zone rather than trading the entire zone blindly.

Internal Zone Structure: The Order Block

Within every supply zone's consolidation, there is a specific candle that IS the order block — the last bearish candle before the bullish move that precedes the drop. This candle is where the highest concentration of institutional sell orders sits. Entering at the body of this specific candle, rather than anywhere in the zone, gives the tightest possible stop and best R:R. (Full detail in Lesson 3.4.)

Fair Value Gaps (FVG) within Supply Zones

An FVG is a three-candle pattern where the middle candle moves so aggressively that it leaves a "gap" between candle 1's high and candle 3's low. Price has a statistical tendency to return and fill this gap before continuing. When an FVG sits within or just below a supply zone, it acts as a magnet pulling price up to the zone for the fill — setting up the short entry perfectly.

Optimal Trade Entry (OTE)

The OTE concept identifies that institutional orders cluster most densely at the 70.5%–79% retracement of the impulsive down-move. Measuring the impulsive leg from the supply zone's high to its low, the OTE sits within that retracement band. Entries at OTE within a supply zone produce the tightest stops and most powerful reactions.

Fibonacci Confluence

Supply zones that align with Fibonacci retracement levels — specifically 0.5, 0.618, and 0.705 — of a prior bullish swing produce the highest-probability short setups. These Fibonacci levels mark where institutional algorithms are programmed to enter, creating self-fulfilling precision.

Advanced Concepts

Partial Mitigation — Price enters the zone but does not fully engulf it; remaining orders are still active and the zone retains value.
Full Mitigation — Price fully traverses the zone from bottom to top; all institutional orders are filled and the zone is exhausted.
OTE (Optimal Trade Entry) — The 70.5%–79% retracement of an impulsive leg where institutional orders are most densely concentrated.
📊 Practical Example

XAUUSD: D1 bearish structure. H4 supply zone at $2,340–$2,360. Within the zone, there is an H4 FVG between $2,348–$2,355. The OTE retracement of the prior drop places the 70.5% level at $2,351. The 0.618 Fibonacci of the prior bullish swing also sits at $2,352. Price rallies into $2,351–$2,352 — the convergence of FVG fill, OTE, and Fibonacci. This is an elite short entry: supply zone + OTE + FVG + Fibonacci = maximum confluence.

💡 Pro Tip

The highest-probability supply zone setup in trading: Supply zone + Bearish market structure + Sell-side liquidity sweep above the zone's high before reversal. The liquidity sweep (a wick above the zone hunting retail stop-losses) is the final confirmation that institutions have filled their sells and are now driving price down. Wait for the sweep; don't enter before it.

Key Takeaway

Supply zones mark where sellers overwhelmed buyers and created a sharp downward move. They're most powerful when fresh (first test), within bearish structure, and aligned with higher timeframe analysis. Mark the full consolidation area, not just a single price level.
