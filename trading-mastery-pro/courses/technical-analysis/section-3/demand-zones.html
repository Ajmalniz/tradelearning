Section 3 · Supply & Demand

Demand Zones Explained

A demand zone is the mirror image of a supply zone — it's where a strong UP-move originated, representing a price area where buyers overwhelmed sellers and caused a sharp rally. When price returns to a demand zone, those buyers are expected to step in again.

Demand zones are found by looking for two things:

  1. A consolidation or base — a few candles moving sideways or slightly down
  2. Followed by a strong impulsive move UP — several large bullish candles surging sharply

The base area — the consolidation before the rally — IS the demand zone. Draw a rectangle over it on your chart.

Key Terms

Demand Zone — A price area where buyers previously overwhelmed sellers, causing a sharp upward move.
Buy Imbalance — More buy orders than sell orders at a price level, creating upward pressure and rapid price movement.
Support — A simplified term for a demand zone; a price level where buying historically overcame selling.
Accumulation — The process of institutions building a long position over time, creating the base before an impulsive rally.
📊 Practical Example

EURUSD on H4 consolidates at 1.0780–1.0790 for 4 candles, then surges 80 pips to 1.0860 in a single session. The 1.0780–1.0790 area is the demand zone. When price later pulls back to that level, traders look for long (buy) setups — expecting buyers to return and push price higher once more.

💡 Pro Tip

Just like supply zones, draw demand zones using candle bodies, not wicks. The body defines where price actually accepted value. Your stop loss should always be placed a few pips below the demand zone's body low — giving the zone room to work without exposing you to excessive risk.

Demand zones represent areas of unfilled institutional buy orders. Large institutions cannot fill their entire long position in one moment — they accumulate across a range of prices, creating the consolidation base you see before the impulsive rally.

Characteristics of a High-Quality Demand Zone

  1. Strong impulsive departure upward — large bullish candles with minimal pullback after the base
  2. First visit (fresh) — never previously tested; each visit to the zone fills more orders and depletes it
  3. Within bullish market structure — trading demand within a bullish trend maximises probability
  4. Higher timeframe confluence — D1 demand zones are ideal for swing trades; H4 for intraday swings

The LBR and RBR Patterns

Two classic demand zone patterns used by intermediate traders:

  • Leave-Base-Rally (LBR): Price drops (leaves), forms a base (consolidates), then rallies strongly. On the return to the base, demand is expected. This is the primary demand zone pattern.
  • Rally-Base-Rally (RBR): Price rallies, forms a base (small pullback/consolidation), then rallies again. This represents a continuation demand zone — valid for adding to an existing long position or entering on a first pullback in a trend.
📊 Practical Example

XAUUSD D1 is in bullish structure. On H4, you see an LBR pattern: price drops to $2,285, consolidates for 3 candles at $2,285–$2,295 (the base), then breaks structure with a rally to $2,340. The $2,285–$2,295 area is the demand zone. On a pullback from $2,340 back to $2,287, you have: D1 bullish structure + fresh H4 demand zone + LBR pattern = high-probability long setup.

💡 Pro Tip

Demand zones formed immediately after a Break of Structure (BOS) carry extra significance. The BOS confirms that institutions stepped in at that exact level to break the prior swing high. The zone IS the footprint of that institutional intervention — treat it as premium demand.

Advanced demand zone analysis moves beyond simply marking the zone — it focuses on internal structure, Fair Value Gaps, Optimal Trade Entry, and stale zone detection to achieve institutional-grade precision.

Internal Structure: The Order Block

Within every demand zone's base, identify the last bullish candle immediately before the impulsive rally. This specific candle is the order block — the highest concentration of institutional buy orders. Rather than entering anywhere in the zone, refining to this candle's body gives you the tightest stop and the best possible risk-to-reward. Full detail is covered in Lesson 3.4.

Fair Value Gap (FVG) Within Demand Zones

An FVG within or just above a demand zone is the space between the high of the last pre-rally candle and the low of the second candle of the impulsive move. Price has a statistical tendency to return and fill this gap — which means the FVG acts as a draw pulling price down into the demand zone. Recognising this tells you why price is coming back, which increases conviction on the long entry.

Optimal Trade Entry (OTE) Within Demand Zones

The 61.8%–70.5% retracement of the impulsive bullish leg (measured from the demand zone's low to the leg's peak) is where institutional buying intensifies on the pullback. Entries within this band, while inside the demand zone, produce the tightest stops and most explosive reactions. Use Fibonacci tools to plot this range precisely.

Stale Zone Detection

Not all demand zones remain valid indefinitely. Signs a zone is being consumed and losing power:

  • Price enters the zone and produces only a small wick (no strong reaction)
  • Price closes inside the zone for multiple candles without a strong bullish rejection
  • After the zone was "tested," the subsequent rally was weak (smaller than the original departure)

Fresh zones produce large, impulsive rejections. Stale or consumed zones produce weak, indecisive reactions — or no reaction at all.

Advanced Concepts

OTE — Optimal Trade Entry; the 61.8%–70.5% retracement zone within an impulsive leg where institutional orders cluster most densely.
Stale Zone — A demand zone tested multiple times with diminishing reactions; institutional orders have been largely filled.
Liquidity Sweep into Demand — Price briefly dips below the demand zone's low (hunting retail stop-losses) before snapping back — the ideal entry signal.
📊 Practical Example

EURUSD H4: Demand zone at 1.0760–1.0775 (LBR pattern, fresh, after BOS). OTE of the prior bullish leg sits at 1.0768. An FVG from the impulsive rally sits between 1.0765–1.0772. Price pulls back to 1.0762, briefly wicking below the zone's low to 1.0758 (liquidity sweep below retail longs), then snaps back sharply. Entry at 1.0763 on H1 bullish close — this is the institutional entry combining demand zone + OTE + FVG fill + liquidity sweep. Stop below 1.0755 (below the sweep wick). Target: prior high at 1.0840. R:R = 1:10.

💡 Pro Tip

The highest-probability long setup in trading: Demand zone + liquidity sweep below it (retail longs stopped out) + immediate bullish reversal candle. The sweep below the zone is the institution collecting liquidity to fill their buy orders. The moment price snaps back above the zone's low, the setup is confirmed. This is the pattern that professional traders wait for — not a simple touch of the zone.

Key Takeaway

Demand zones mark where buyers overpowered sellers and launched a sharp rally. The best demand zones are fresh (never visited), within bullish structure, and ideally confirmed by a BOS immediately after the zone formed. Always define your stop loss clearly below the zone's low.
