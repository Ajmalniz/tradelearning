Section 2 · Market Structure

What is Market Structure

Price Moves in Waves

One of the most important things to understand about markets is that price never moves in a straight line. Even during the strongest uptrend, price will rise, pull back slightly, rise again, pull back again — and so on. This wave-like motion is not random. It is the result of buyers and sellers constantly exchanging control, and it creates a repeating pattern that we call market structure.

Market structure is simply the series of peaks and valleys that price leaves behind as it moves. By reading those peaks and valleys — identifying whether each new peak is higher or lower than the last, and whether each new valley is higher or lower than the last — you can determine the dominant direction of the market at any point in time.

The Four Building Blocks of Structure

There are four key labels you need to learn. Every trending market is described entirely using these four terms:

  • HH — Higher High: A peak that is higher than the previous peak. Each HH means buyers pushed price above where they pushed it last time — a sign of bullish strength.
  • HL — Higher Low: A trough that is higher than the previous trough. Even when price pulls back, it doesn't fall as far as it did last time — buyers are stepping in earlier. This is the hallmark of an uptrend.
  • LH — Lower High: A peak that is lower than the previous peak. Buyers are running out of conviction — they can't push price as high as before. This signals bearish pressure building.
  • LL — Lower Low: A trough that is lower than the previous trough. Sellers are in control, pushing price to new lows on each move down.

How an Uptrend Looks in Structure

In a clear uptrend, price will create this sequence: HL → HH → HL → HH → HL → HH. Notice the pattern: every rally makes a Higher High, and every pullback forms a Higher Low. As long as this sequence continues, the uptrend is intact and you should be looking for opportunities to buy, not sell.

In a downtrend the opposite holds: LH → LL → LH → LL → LH → LL. Each rally is weaker than the last (LH), and each selloff pushes price to a new low (LL).

Key Terms

Market Structure — The pattern of peaks (highs) and troughs (lows) that price creates as it moves, used to determine trend direction and bias.
Higher High (HH) — A swing high that exceeds the previous swing high. Indicates bullish momentum.
Higher Low (HL) — A swing low that is above the previous swing low. Confirms buyers remain in control during pullbacks.
Lower High (LH) — A swing high that fails to reach the previous swing high. Indicates weakening bullish momentum or growing bearish pressure.
Lower Low (LL) — A swing low that falls below the previous swing low. Confirms sellers are in control.
Swing Point — Any significant peak or trough on the chart where price reversed direction. The raw material of market structure analysis.
📊 Practical Example — XAUUSD H4

Imagine you're looking at a Gold (XAUUSD) 4-hour chart. You observe the following sequence of price moves:

Step 1: Price rallies to a peak at $2,290 — this becomes your first reference swing high.

Step 2: Price pulls back and finds support at $2,245 — this is a swing low.

Step 3: Price rallies again and reaches $2,320 — a new high, higher than $2,290. This is your HH.

Step 4: Price retraces to $2,270 — a new low, but higher than $2,245. This is your HL. The uptrend is confirmed.

Step 5: Price then pushes to $2,355 — another HH. The trend continues.

At this point, any trader looking at this chart should be bullish on Gold. The structure clearly shows buyers are in control. The correct strategy is to wait for the next HL to form — a pullback toward the $2,290–$2,300 area — and look for a buying opportunity there.

Internal vs External Structure

Once you understand the basics of HH, HL, LH, and LL, the next step is learning that market structure exists on multiple levels simultaneously. A single chart contains both a large-scale story (the external structure) and a smaller-scale story happening within it (the internal structure). Skilled traders read both.

External Structure

External structure refers to the major swing points visible on higher timeframes — typically the Daily (D1) or 4-Hour (H4) charts. These are the peaks and troughs that define the dominant trend. External structure moves are large, they take days or weeks to complete, and they represent the decisions of the biggest players in the market — institutional traders, central banks, and large funds.

External structure gives you your directional bias. If the D1 external structure is bullish (HH/HL), your overall bias for the coming days is bullish. You should be looking for long setups, not shorts. This is non-negotiable: trading against your external structure bias is one of the most common mistakes retail traders make.

Internal Structure

Internal structure refers to the smaller waves that form within a single external structure move. When you zoom into H1 or M15, you'll see that what looks like a single clean "leg up" on the H4 is actually composed of many smaller HH/HL cycles. This internal structure is where you find your entry timing.

Here is the powerful combination: use external structure (D1/H4) to determine bias, then switch to internal structure (H1/M15) to find the precise moment price is ready to continue in the direction of the external trend. You want to see the internal structure align with your external bias before pulling the trigger.

Multi-Timeframe Alignment

The concept of timeframe alignment is critical. The strongest trade setups occur when multiple timeframes all tell the same structural story. When D1 is bullish, H4 is bullish, and H1 just formed an HH, you have triple alignment — the highest probability condition for a long trade.

📊 Practical Example — Multi-Timeframe Structure

D1 Chart: Gold has been making HH and HL for the past 3 weeks. External structure is clearly bullish.

H4 Chart: Within the D1 uptrend, H4 shows a recent pullback — price made a lower swing over the last 2 days. This is the normal corrective phase within a larger uptrend.

H1 Chart (Internal Structure): On the H1, you watch the corrective move and wait. When H1 forms a new Higher High (breaking above the most recent LH in the correction), the internal structure has shifted back to bullish — signaling that the correction is likely over and the D1 uptrend is resuming.

Trade Action: You enter long on the H1 BOS (break of the swing high in the correction), with your stop below the most recent H1 HL and your target at the next D1 swing high target.

💡 Pro Tip — Multi-Timeframe Alignment

Never take an entry purely on a single timeframe. Before entering any trade, check at least two timeframes: one for bias (the higher timeframe external structure) and one for entry timing (the lower timeframe internal structure). When both point in the same direction, your probability of a winning trade increases significantly. When they conflict, reduce size or stand aside entirely.

Institutional Order Flow and the Creation of Structure

At the expert level, market structure is not just a pattern to recognize — it is the footprint of institutional order flow. Every swing high and swing low you see on a chart was created by deliberate buying or selling decisions made by banks, hedge funds, and large institutional players. Understanding why structure forms the way it does gives you a massive edge over traders who simply memorize patterns.

Impulsive vs Corrective Phases

Every structural move consists of two alternating phases:

Impulsive Phase: A large, fast, directional move driven by institutional order flow. On a candlestick chart, impulsive moves are characterized by large-bodied candles with small wicks, minimal overlap between consecutive candles, and a clear sense of urgency. Institutions are executing large orders, and price moves quickly to fill them. Impulsive phases create the HH or LL in a trend.

Corrective Phase: A slower, overlapping, choppy move in the opposite direction. Corrections are where institutions are either pausing (allowing retail to take the other side of their position) or gradually re-accumulating before the next impulsive leg. Corrections create the HL or LH in a trend. They tend to be smaller in both size and speed than the impulsive move that preceded them.

A healthy trend will have impulsive moves that are significantly larger than the corrective moves. When corrections start to match or exceed the size of impulsive moves, it is a warning that the trend is weakening.

Wyckoff: The Underlying Mechanism

Richard Wyckoff's accumulation and distribution schematics provide the deepest explanation for why market structure forms the way it does. In Wyckoff's framework, large operators (what we now call "smart money" or institutions) must go through a careful process of building or exiting positions without moving price against themselves:

Accumulation: Before an uptrend begins, institutions must buy large quantities of an asset. They do this during a sideways "consolidation" range. The structure during accumulation appears choppy — neither clear HH/HL nor LH/LL. This is by design. Retail traders, confused by the sideways action, give up and sell, providing the liquidity institutions need to build their longs.

Markup (Uptrend): Once accumulation is complete, institutions drive price higher, creating the HH/HL structure we associate with uptrends. Each HL is a point where institutions add to their position or allow latecomers to enter.

Distribution: Near major highs, institutions begin exiting. Again, the structure appears choppy and sideways. Retail traders, seeing "resistance" but also an uptrend, are confused. Institutions are selling into retail buying.

Markdown (Downtrend): Once distribution is complete, price falls in a LH/LL structure. Retail longs who bought near the highs are trapped and eventually capitulate, accelerating the decline.

Market Structure Shifts (MSS) as Trade Signals

A Market Structure Shift (MSS) occurs when price breaks a significant swing point in the opposite direction of the current trend — often after a liquidity sweep above a swing high (in a downtrend) or below a swing low (in an uptrend). The MSS is not just a warning signal; for expert traders, it is a primary entry trigger when combined with order flow confirmation.

The sequence looks like this: In a downtrend, price sweeps above a previous LH (taking out buy-side stops), then reverses aggressively and breaks below the most recent HL. This double confirmation — liquidity sweep followed by structural break — is one of the highest-probability setups in technical analysis.

💡 Pro Tip — Structure Shifts as Trade Entries

Don't wait for full trend confirmation before entering on an MSS. The best risk-to-reward setups come from entering early in the new structure, not after it has already made 3 HH/HL cycles. Look for: (1) a clear impulsive move that breaks the last swing point, (2) a corrective pullback that holds above/below the break point, and (3) a resumption of the new direction. Enter on the resumption with your stop just beyond the structural break level. This gives you tight stops and expansive targets.

Key Takeaway

Market structure is the foundation of all technical analysis. Before looking at any setup, establish whether you're in a bullish (HH/HL) or bearish (LH/LL) structure — this determines your trade bias for the session.
