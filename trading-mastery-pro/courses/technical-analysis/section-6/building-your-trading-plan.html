Section 6 · Putting It All Together

Building Your Trading Plan

A trading plan is your personal rulebook — a written document that governs every aspect of your trading activity. It covers what you trade, when you trade, how much you risk, what setups you take, and what you do when things go wrong. Without it, every trading session starts from scratch with no consistent framework guiding your decisions.

A trading plan does not guarantee profits. It does guarantee consistency — and consistency is what allows you to identify and fix problems, build real skill, and eventually achieve profitability. Traders without plans cannot learn from experience because every session is a different improvised experiment.

Key Terms

Trading Plan — A written personal document specifying: markets traded, timeframes, entry rules, risk parameters, session rules, and journaling requirements.
Trading Journal — A systematic record of every trade: date, instrument, entry, exit, outcome, and notes on execution quality and emotional state.
Edge — A statistical advantage that, when applied consistently over many trades, produces a positive expected return.
📊 Minimal Beginner Trading Plan

Markets: EURUSD and XAUUSD only.
Timeframes: Daily (bias) → H4 (zones) → H1 (entry).
Setup: HTF bullish/bearish structure + price at demand/supply zone + H1 confirmation candle.
Risk: 1% per trade maximum. No more than 2 trades open at once.
Sessions: London (3–6am EST) and New York (8–11am EST) only.
Daily Stop: Stop trading after 2 losses in one day.
Journaling: Log every trade before ending the session.

A complete intermediate trading plan has six sections:

Section 1 — Markets and Timeframes
List the specific instruments you will trade. Start with 2–3 maximum. Specify your analysis timeframes: HTF for bias, MTF for zone identification, LTF for entry trigger. Consistency across instruments and timeframes is what builds pattern recognition.

Section 2 — Setup Rules
Define exactly what constitutes a valid setup. For example: "I only enter if: (a) Daily structure is bullish/bearish, (b) price is at a pre-marked H4 OB or S&D zone, (c) a liquidity sweep has occurred at or near the zone, and (d) a H1 CHoCH or BOS confirms direction." Every criterion must be binary — yes or no — to prevent subjective interpretation.

Section 3 — Risk Rules
Risk per trade: 1%. Maximum daily loss: 2–3% (stop trading for the day). Maximum open positions: 2 (or 3 if non-correlated). Minimum R:R: 1:2 on every trade. These are hard limits — not guidelines.

Section 4 — Session and Timing Rules
Which hours you trade and which you do not. No trading during major news events (CPI, NFP, Fed rate decisions). No trading during the Asian session dead zone (10pm–2am EST) unless a specific setup triggers. These rules prevent low-probability entries driven by boredom rather than analysis.

Section 5 — Trade Management Rules
When (if ever) you move your SL to breakeven. Your partial close targets if using scale-out. Your maximum hold time. Your rules for exiting early — and the default should be "no early exits unless setup is fundamentally invalidated on the higher timeframe."

Section 6 — Journaling Requirements
Record for every trade: date, instrument, entry price, SL, TP, lot size, outcome (pips + dollars), whether setup met all criteria (yes/no), and your emotional state at entry. Review weekly. Calculate win rate, average R:R, and equity curve. Adjust the plan quarterly based on data, not emotions.

💡 Pro Tip

Write your first trading plan today — even if it is rough. A bad written plan beats a perfect mental plan. The act of writing forces you to make decisions you have been avoiding: exactly which setups, exactly which markets, exactly which risk limits. You can refine it as you learn, but you cannot refine something that only exists in your head.

An expert trading plan adds quantitative performance targets, adaptive rules, and quarterly review protocols:

Performance Targets (realistic, not aspirational):
Monthly target: +3–5% on account balance.
Weekly target: Not meaningful — focus on monthly.
Maximum monthly drawdown: 5% (if hit, reduce to paper trading for one week).
Minimum 50 trades before evaluating strategy performance statistically.

Adaptive Risk Rules:
Win rate >55% over last 20 trades: allowed to add one additional instrument to watchlist.
Drawdown >10%: reduce position size to 0.5% until equity recovers to -5%.
Three consecutive losing weeks: take a 3-day break from live trading, review journal.

Quarterly Review Protocol:
Calculate: total trades, win rate, average winner (R), average loser (R), max consecutive losses, max drawdown, monthly P&L.
Ask: Which setup type has the highest win rate? Which instrument performs best? Which session produces the most consistent results? Which emotional patterns appear before losing trades?
Adjust: Remove underperforming setups. Increase weighting toward consistently profitable patterns.

Journal Metrics to Track

Win Rate — Percentage of trades that were profitable. Target: not maximized, but above the break-even rate for your R:R.
Average R per trade — (Total R gained − Total R lost) ÷ Total trades. Positive number = profitable strategy.
Max Consecutive Losses — The longest losing streak in your sample. Plan your psychology for this — it will happen.
Equity Curve — A line chart of your account balance over time. Smooth upward slope = consistent edge. Choppy or declining = review required.

💡 Pro Tip

Treat your trading plan as a living document, not a fixed contract. It should evolve as your data accumulates. But only change it based on your journal data — not based on a bad week's emotions. The rule: never make plan changes during a drawdown. Wait until you are at a neutral or positive equity position, then review calmly and make reasoned adjustments.

Key Takeaway

A trading plan is your operating manual. Cover markets, setup rules, risk parameters, session rules, trade management, and journaling requirements. Write it, follow it, review it quarterly, and update it only based on data — not emotions. This document is the difference between a gambler and a trader.
