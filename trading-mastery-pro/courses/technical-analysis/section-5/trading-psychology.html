Section 5 · Risk Management & Position Sizing

Trading Psychology: Fear & Greed

The biggest enemy in trading is not the market — it is your own emotions. Fear and greed are hardwired into human psychology from millions of years of evolution. Fear kept our ancestors alive by avoiding threats. Greed drove them to secure more resources. In the jungle, these instincts worked perfectly. In trading, they destroy accounts systematically.

Fear causes you to exit winning trades far too early, freeze on valid setups, and refuse to pull the trigger even when everything lines up. Greed causes you to hold losing trades "hoping" they reverse, chase moves you already missed, and take oversized positions because "this one is guaranteed." These are universal feelings every trader experiences. The difference between profitable and losing traders is not the absence of these emotions — it is having rules that prevent them from making trading decisions.

Key Terms

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) — Entering a trade because price is moving fast and you fear missing the opportunity. Results in late, high-risk entries near exhaustion.
Revenge Trading — Taking impulsive trades immediately after a loss to "win back" what was lost. Almost always results in additional losses.
Discipline — Consistently following your trading rules regardless of emotional state, recent wins, or recent losses.
Trading Plan — A written set of specific rules governing which setups you take, your risk parameters, and your daily stop limits.
📊 Practical Example — FOMO in Action

You watch EURUSD surge 200 pips during the London session without you in it. Your setup required a pullback to an OB that never came. Now you enter long near the top of the move purely because it's "going up fast." This is FOMO — you entered at the worst possible risk point, chasing a move that's already 90% done. The market does not care that you missed the move. There will always be another setup.

The three most destructive psychological patterns in trading — and why they happen:

1. Moving your stop loss further away. "It'll come back." This converts a defined, controlled loss into a potentially catastrophic one. The impulse is strong because cutting the loss feels like admitting you were wrong — and humans are deeply averse to being wrong. But the stop exists to enforce your pre-trade risk calculation. Moving it is not analytical; it is emotional override of your own plan. This single behavior is responsible for the majority of account blowups.

2. Exiting winners too early. A trade is at +80 pips and your target is +150 pips. Fear of "giving it back" causes you to close. Over hundreds of trades, this consistently destroys your planned R:R. The math that made your strategy profitable evaporates because you cannot let winners run. Every early exit is a deduction from your expected value.

3. Revenge trading after a loss. The emotional impulse to immediately recover a loss causes impulsive, unplanned trades taken out of anger rather than analysis. These trades almost always lack the necessary confluence, are oversized, and result in additional losses — compounding the original damage.

💡 Pro Tip

After any losing trade, implement a mandatory 30-minute break before considering your next trade. Stand up, leave the screen, do something else. The emotional impulse to recover immediately is one of the most reliably destructive forces in trading. The discipline to pause and reset is worth more than any technical analysis skill.

Performance psychology research demonstrates that trading decision quality is inversely correlated with emotional arousal. The autonomic nervous system's fight-or-flight response physically impairs the prefrontal cortex — the brain region responsible for rational, probabilistic decision-making. Under stress, the brain defaults to fast, pattern-matching heuristics that were useful for survival but are systematically harmful for financial decision-making.

Practical protocols used by professional traders to maintain prefrontal dominance:

Pre-session calibration: A structured pre-market routine — 5 minutes of breathing, reviewing yesterday's trades, stating the day's plan — activates the prefrontal cortex before the market opens. Professionals treat this as non-negotiable, equivalent to warming up before athletic performance.

Hard daily loss limit: Stop trading for the remainder of the day after losing 2–3% of account. Not "try to stop" — close the platform completely. Losing sessions create emotional feedback loops that progressively degrade decision quality. The best traders know that the ability to stop and preserve capital for the next day is itself a skill.

Process-oriented journaling: Record not just the trade details but your emotional state: "felt FOMO," "hesitated 8 minutes," "very confident — maybe overconfident." After 100+ entries, patterns emerge that reveal your specific behavioral triggers — the pairs, sessions, and conditions that most reliably produce emotional overrides of your plan.

Advanced Concepts

Prefrontal Cortex — The brain region responsible for rational decision-making, planning, and impulse control — easily impaired by emotional stress.
Process Orientation — Evaluating trades based on rule adherence, not profit/loss outcomes. A loss from a well-executed plan is a good trade. A win from an impulsive entry is a bad trade that got lucky.
Cognitive Bias — Systematic patterns of irrational judgment. Loss aversion, recency bias, and overconfidence are the most common in trading.
📊 Two Traders, Same Losing Streak

Both traders lose 3 trades in a row. Trader A gets angry, doubles position size on the next trade to "make it back fast," loses again at double risk, then triples size — account is now in a 20% drawdown. Trader B reviews each loss in their journal, confirms all three followed the plan exactly, notes the streak as within statistical expectation, and takes the fourth setup at normal 1% risk. Trader B's account is down 3%. Trader A's is down 20%. Same market, same losing streak. Completely different outcomes due to psychology alone.

💡 Pro Tip

Trade the smallest position size you can be genuinely emotionally neutral about. If the thought of losing on a trade would meaningfully affect your mood or day, your position is too large. Scale down until a loss feels like the cost of doing business — because that is exactly what it is. Emotional neutrality is a skill you build through small-size practice before scaling up.

Key Takeaway

Fear and greed are universal — every trader feels them. The solution is not willpower; it is systems. A written trading plan removes decisions, properly sized positions keep you emotionally neutral, and hard daily loss limits prevent emotional spirals from becoming account-destroying events.
