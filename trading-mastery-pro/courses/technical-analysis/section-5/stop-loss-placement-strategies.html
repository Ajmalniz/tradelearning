Section 5 · Risk Management & Position Sizing

Stop Loss Placement Strategies

A stop loss is an automatic order that closes your trade at a predetermined price to limit how much you can lose. Without a stop loss, a losing trade can run unchecked and destroy your account overnight. Every trade you take must have a stop loss set before you click the entry button — not after, and not "I'll watch it manually."

Stop losses should be placed where your trade idea is proven wrong — not at a round number, not 20 pips away "because that's comfortable." Ask yourself: "If price reaches this level, is my original trade thesis still valid?" If the answer is no, that is where your stop goes.

Key Terms

Stop Loss (SL) — An order that automatically closes your position when price reaches a specified level against you.
Mental Stop — Deciding in your head when to exit without placing a real order. Unreliable — do not use this.
Hard Stop — A real order placed in the market at your stop price. Always use a hard stop.
Invalidation Point — The price level at which your original trade thesis is definitively proven wrong.
📊 Practical Example

You buy EURUSD because price reached a demand zone below the previous swing low at 1.0850. Your trade idea: "buyers entered here and will push price higher." If price breaks significantly below the demand zone — say to 1.0820 — your demand zone has failed. That is your invalidation point. Stop loss goes at 1.0815, just below the structural low, giving a small buffer for wicks.

Structure-based stop placement is the professional standard. There are three primary methods, all sharing the same logic: the SL goes beyond the structural level that validates your trade.

Method 1 — Beyond the Swing High/Low: If buying at a swing low, your SL goes a few pips below the lowest point of that swing. If that low is broken on a candle close, the swing low has failed and the trade is invalid.

Method 2 — Beyond the Order Block: If entering off a bullish order block, your SL goes below the low of the OB candle. A close below the OB means the order block has been broken and is no longer valid.

Method 3 — Beyond the Liquidity Sweep: If price sweeps a swing low and reverses (stop hunt setup), your SL goes just below the wick of the sweep candle — the very bottom of the spike. If price revisits and breaks below that wick, the reversal has definitively failed.

💡 Pro Tip

Always add a 5–10 pip buffer beyond the structural level. A SL placed exactly at the swing low will frequently get hit by a single spike wick before price reverses in your direction. The buffer absorbs spread, execution slippage, and normal wick behavior. It costs a few extra pips of risk — offset by reducing lot size accordingly — but drastically reduces the number of valid setups stopped out by noise.

Advanced stop placement incorporates volatility-adjusted sizing using ATR (Average True Range). If XAUUSD has a 14-period ATR of 180 pips on the H4, placing a 25-pip stop loss is statistically guaranteed to be hit by random market noise — the instrument regularly moves 180+ pips within a single H4 candle. Your structural SL must account for the instrument's normal volatility.

A volatility-adjusted rule: your SL should be at minimum 1.0× ATR from your entry price. If H4 ATR is 180 pips, a SL closer than 180 pips is in "noise territory" and will be stopped out by random fluctuation before the trade can develop. This forces you to use a wider SL (and correspondingly smaller position size to maintain the same dollar risk) or wait for a more precise LTF entry within the same HTF zone.

Advanced Concepts

ATR (Average True Range) — A volatility indicator measuring the average range of price movement over N periods. ATR < SL distance = structural validity. ATR > SL distance = likely to be hit by noise.
Trailing Stop — A SL that moves in the direction of your trade as it becomes profitable, locking in gains while remaining in the position.
Breakeven Stop — Moving your SL to your exact entry price once the trade is sufficiently in profit, eliminating potential loss on the trade.
📊 ATR-Adjusted Stop — XAUUSD Example

XAUUSD H4 ATR = 180 pips. You identify a demand zone at 1920.00, nearest swing low at 1918.50. Structure-based SL (below swing low + 5-pip buffer) = 1913.00. ATR-based minimum SL = 1920.00 − $18.00 = 1902.00. The ATR says the structural SL at 1913 is at risk of being hit by noise. Solution: drill down to H1 for a refined entry within the same zone, allowing the structure-based SL to respect both the zone AND the ATR volatility reality.

💡 Pro Tip

The single most dangerous habit in trading: moving your stop loss away from the trade as price approaches it. This converts a controlled, defined loss into an unlimited one. If price is near your stop, it means your trade thesis is being tested. Either it holds and reverses (you're fine) or it breaks your stop (you take the loss as planned). Never widen a stop that's being approached.

Key Takeaway

Stop losses belong at points of structural invalidation — the price at which your trade thesis is proven wrong. Never place a stop at an arbitrary distance or a round number. Structure-based SL placement is your primary tool; ATR ensures you account for each instrument's actual volatility.
