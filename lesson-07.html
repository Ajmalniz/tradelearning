The Bid Reload Pattern

The Bid Reload is one of the most important signals on the order book. It occurs when a large passive order suddenly appears at a price level during a consolidation or compression phase — where price has been moving sideways or with small range.

BID RELOAD

Sudden Large Passive Order Appears

Imagine price is consolidating between two levels. Then suddenly, 110 contracts appear on the bid at the bottom of the range. This is a Bid Reload.

What happens next? The market's algorithms front-run this order. Because everyone can see 110 passive buy contracts at −3, algo systems immediately buy in anticipation — they know price is very likely to be supported there and will potentially bounce or accelerate.

How to Use the Bid Reload in Trading

Scenario: You have a long bias for the day based on VWAP and CVD.

Signal: You see a big bid reload at a level that aligns with your expected support area.

Action: This is amazing validation. Enter long with your stop below the reload level. The 110-contract wall is your protection. If it holds (absorbs sellers and bounces), you ride the move up.

Key insight: "Seeing this kind of information, it's an amazing information to validate your idea." — Fabio

⚠ SPOOFING

The Fake Reload (Market Manipulation)

Spoofing is when someone places a large passive order — like that 110-contract bid reload — but has absolutely no intention of being filled. The goal is to manipulate other traders and algorithms into trading in a direction, so the spoofer can then exit at a better price.

How Spoofing Works

1. A large player wants to SHORT the market
2. They place a massive 500-contract BID at a low level (looks like huge support)
3. Retail traders and algos see it → think "big buyer here!" → they BUY
4. Price rises due to these new buyers
5. Spoofer CANCELS their fake bid and SELLS into the buying pressure at the higher price
6. Price then drops because the "support" never existed

This is illegal but still happens in real markets daily. The SEC and CFTC prosecute it, but detection is difficult.

✅ Real Reload (Institutional)

Order stays at the level as price approaches. Gets absorbed (partially filled). Level holds multiple times. Usually part of a pattern with aggressive orders in same direction.

⚠ Spoof (Manipulation)

Order disappears just before price reaches it. No absorption. Price moves OPPOSITE to what the order suggested. Order was placed when price was far away and cancelled as it approached.

"Be careful also because this pattern is used a lot by spoofing... there is a market manipulation technique where they put this order here but they don't really intend to be filled — to manipulate the movement above to short from a better level. Spoofing is illegal but still used every day." — Fabio
ICEBERG

Hidden Liquidity — The Invisible Wall

The iceberg order is what Fabio calls "invisible liquidity." Here's why it's invisible and why it's incredibly important:

On the heat map, you might see only 8 contracts at a level. That looks tiny — easily consumed by a few aggressive orders. You'd expect price to blow right through it.

But what if every time those 8 contracts get consumed, they are immediately reloaded by an algorithm? Again and again, hundreds of times? That "8 contract" level could actually represent 500+ contracts total — but you never see more than 8 at once.

🔄
Hidden Reality: 500 reloads happenSellers consume 8 → 8 reloads. Sellers consume 8 → 8 reloads. This repeats 60+ times = 480+ contracts total absorbed.
🎯
Detected by: Iceberg Detection IndicatorsSpecial indicators track the reloading activity. When they flag a level, even if only 8 show, you KNOW there are hundreds behind it.

Why This Matters Enormously

Sellers tried to break through a level. They consumed 8 contracts. Then 8 more. Then 8 more. The level NEVER broke. Eventually they exhausted their selling pressure and price reversed upward.

Without an iceberg detector, you watched price "hold at a tiny 8-contract level" and thought: "weird, why didn't it break?" WITH the detector, you know: "there were 500 hidden contracts there — no wonder it held."

This changes your understanding of key levels completely.

Example from Fabio's live session: "You can see that they did a reload here and you had this arrow showing you where the sellers found a wall from the iceberg — and even if the liquidity, the resting liquidity, was not so high, this one was a super important level."

Institutional distribution using icebergs: Institutions needing to sell a large position place multiple iceberg orders on the way up. Each one absorbs buying pressure and fills sell orders. When all are filled, price collapses.
