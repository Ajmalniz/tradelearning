Path of Least Resistance:
Where Price Goes Next

Learn to calculate which direction offers the least obstacle for price — the book slope method that professionals use for every trading decision.

⏱ 12 min 🟡 Intermediate 📐 Quantitative

The Core Concept

If you fix a constant amount of "effort" (aggressive force), price will move further in the direction where there is less passive liquidity. More resistance above = price moves down more easily. More resistance below = price moves up more easily.

This is the concept of the Path of Least Resistance — the direction where a given force causes the most price movement.

"The path of least resistance is down. Why? Because to go above, you need to fulfill 273 passive orders. The total limit above the price is 273. Below the price is 122. So fixing the effort, it's easier to move price down." — Fabio

The Book Slope: Visual Intuition

Think of passive liquidity as a hill. The steeper the hill on one side, the harder it is to push price in that direction. The flatter/smaller the hill on the other side, the easier price rolls.

How to Use Path of Least Resistance in Trading

This concept has direct, practical applications at every timeframe:

Short-Term Scalping Decisions

When you're deciding whether to enter a long or a short, quickly scan the heat map. If there are 3× more passive sell orders above than buy orders below in the next 5 levels — the probability strongly favors downward movement. Don't fight the slope.

Breakout Confirmation

Before entering a breakout trade, check: has the passive resistance been consumed? If price just broke through a massive sell wall (273 contracts) and now the levels above are thin (only 40 contracts), the path of least resistance has just shifted upward. Now is the time to join.

Stop Loss Placement

Place your stop loss BELOW a major passive buy cluster (for long trades) or ABOVE a major passive sell cluster (for short trades). The cluster acts as your "protection wall" — if price can't break through it, your trade is safe.

Remember: price always finds the path of least resistance. Institutional players and algorithmic systems use this principle constantly. When you can read the book slope, you're reading the same data they are.

⚡ Key Takeaways from Lesson 5

  • Path of least resistance = the direction where the same "effort" (aggressive contracts) moves price the furthest.
  • Calculate it: sum passive liquidity above (5 levels) and below (5 levels). Smaller total = direction of least resistance.
  • 273 above vs 122 below → price is 2.2× more likely to move down.
  • The book slope visualizes this: like a hill — price rolls down the gentler slope.
  • Use it for: entry direction, breakout confirmation, and stop loss placement.
  • Always check the slope BEFORE entering a trade — you want to be aligned with least resistance, not fighting it.

✅ 180 above vs 60 below = 3× more resistance going up. The same 50-contract effort would move price 3× further downward. Path of least resistance: DOWN.

2. What does "fixing the effort" mean in the path of least resistance calculation?

A) Setting a fixed stop loss distance
B) Assuming the same number of aggressive contracts attacks in each direction, so you can compare passive resistance on each side fairly
C) Fixing a price target
D) Setting a maximum position size
✅ By "fixing" the effort (e.g., 50 contracts), you create a fair comparison — same aggressive force in each direction. Then you compare resistance to see which direction yields more movement.

3. Price is near a level with 400 passive buy contracts below and only 80 passive sell contracts above. For a scalper, this suggests:

A) Go short — those 400 contracts are likely sell orders
B) The path of least resistance is UP — only 80 sell contracts blocking the way, much less than the 400 buy contracts below that would absorb selling pressure
C) Don't trade — too much liquidity makes it unpredictable

✅ 80 contracts above vs 400 below = path of least resistance is strongly UP. Price will face minimal resistance going up, and massive support preventing downward movement.
