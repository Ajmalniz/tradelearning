Phase 2 · Lesson 6

Context Tools:
CVD, VWAP & Volume Profile

The supporting indicators that give you directional bias and session context — how professionals pair these with the heat map for high-conviction trades.

⏱ 15 min 🟡 Intermediate 📊 Indicators

Why You Need Context Tools

The liquidity heat map shows you WHERE the orders are. But it doesn't tell you the broader context — who is dominating the session, whether buyers or sellers have taken control, or where the "fair value" of price is for the day.

These three context tools fill that gap. Together with the heat map, they give you a complete picture before taking any trade.

CVD

Cumulative Volume Delta

Who is winning the battle — buyers or sellers?

CVD (Cumulative Volume Delta) measures the net difference between aggressive buying volume and aggressive selling volume, accumulated over time.

Formula: CVD = Σ(Buy aggression) − Σ(Sell aggression)

CVD Reading Guide
↑ CVD Rising

More aggressive buyers than sellers. Buyers dominating. Confirms bullish price movement.

↓ CVD Falling

More aggressive sellers than buyers. Sellers dominating. Confirms bearish price movement.

⚠ Price Up, CVD Flat

Bullish divergence signal: price rising but buyers NOT aggressive. Move is weak. Potential reversal warning.

⚠ CVD Flipping Lower

Even if directional bias is long for the day — sellers are taking short-term control. Fabio says: "I will not try a reversal play while sellers dominate. It's a suicide."

Fabio's CVD Rule

"What does it mean that I will not try a reversal play? I will not try to get long while the sellers are dominating the auction because it's a suicide. I will wait for the buyers to show me some kind of protection."

In other words: never trade against the CVD direction in the short term. Wait for the CVD to align with your intended trade direction.

VWAP

Volume Weighted Average Price

The "fair value" of price for today's session

VWAP is the average price of all transactions during the day, weighted by volume. It represents the "fair value" of price — where most business has been transacted.

VWAP Standard Deviation Levels
+2 Std Dev
Extremely extended. Buyers very stretched. Often reversal zone.
+1 Std Dev
Price is above fair value. Buyers in control but getting stretched.
VWAP
Fair value. Price magnetically attracted back here during balance.
−1 Std Dev
Price is below fair value. Sellers in control but getting stretched.
−2 Std Dev
Extremely extended. Sellers very stretched. Often bounce zone.

From the live session: "We can see that we are above the first standard deviation of VWAP. So buyers are definitely in control. This is the fair value of the day."

How to Use VWAP in Practice

Above VWAP: Buyers in control. Bias is long. Look for long setups. Short setups have less probability.

Below VWAP: Sellers in control. Bias is short. Look for short setups.

At VWAP: Fair value. Market may rebalance. Wait for direction to develop.

After heat map confirms: Once price hits your target heat map level, check if it aligns with a VWAP standard deviation level. When they coincide, the probability of reaction is significantly higher.

VP

Volume Profile (Horizontal Volume)

Where the most trading activity actually happened

The Volume Profile shows how much volume was traded at each price level — displayed as horizontal bars on the chart. Price levels with the most volume are called High Volume Nodes (HVN) — the market "accepted" price there. Low volume areas are called Low Volume Nodes (LVN) — price moved through quickly.

  • 🟢
    High Volume Nodes (HVN) = strong support/resistance — these are price levels the market revisits repeatedly
  • ⚡
    Low Volume Nodes (LVN) = price accelerates through these — when price enters a thin zone, it moves fast
  • 📍
    Point of Control (POC) = the single price level with most volume — the anchor of the session's fair value range
  • 🔍
    Volume Spread Analysis (VSA): Look for aggressive bubbles at volume cluster edges — these mark major decision points where one side took clear control

Fabio from the live session: "For example, this aggression here got completely stopped by the relevant [volume level]. And also what you are seeing at the moment — a lot of sell aggression is trying to flip the auction."

Combining All Three: The Full Context Framework

These tools work best together. Here's how to use them as a unified context layer before reading the heat map:

🌊

Step 1: VWAP Position

Is price above or below VWAP? This sets your primary bias for the session.

📈

Step 2: CVD Direction

Is the CVD rising or falling? This confirms (or warns against) your VWAP bias in real time.

📊

Step 3: Volume Profile

Where are the HVN and LVN zones? Do they align with heat map levels?

🔥

Step 4: Heat Map

NOW read the heat map. All three above gave you context — now find the exact entry with the heat map patterns.

Fabio's live example: "Buyers are in complete control. You can see here buyers are dominating this auction from the beginning of the session. We can go on the VWAP that is giving you also the standard deviation of where we are. And if we zoom out, we can see that we are above the first standard deviation of VWAP. So buyers are definitely in control. This is the fair value of the day."

⚡ Key Takeaways from Lesson 6

  • CVD measures net aggressive buying vs selling. Rising = buyers winning. Falling = sellers winning.
  • Never trade against the CVD direction in the short term — "it's a suicide."
  • VWAP is the session's "fair value." Above = buyers in control. Below = sellers in control.
  • VWAP standard deviations mark extended / stretched conditions where reversals are likely.
  • Volume Profile shows accepted price zones (HVN) and fast-move zones (LVN).
  • Use VWAP → CVD → Volume Profile → Heat Map in sequence to build conviction before entering.

Knowledge Check

1. CVD is falling while price is still moving upward. This is:

A) A bearish divergence — buyers are weak and the upward move is not supported by aggressive buying. A reversal may be near.
B) A bullish signal — sellers are falling means there are fewer sellers
C) A normal market condition with no meaning
D) Confirmation that price will continue up
✅ CVD falling while price rises = bearish divergence. Price is moving up but NOT supported by aggressive buying. This is a weak move — often followed by reversal. Fabio warns against going long in this condition.

2. Price is above VWAP +1 standard deviation. What does this tell you?

A) Price is at fair value and equilibrium
B) This is a strong buy signal — keep going long
C) Buyers are in control but price is extended above fair value — be cautious about new longs, potential retracement to VWAP is likely
D) Price will definitely reverse because it's above VWAP
✅ Above VWAP = buyers in control, but above +1 std dev = stretched. Price tends to revert toward VWAP. Take partial profits and be cautious about adding new long positions at this extension.

3. In Fabio's framework, what is the correct order for building a trade bias?

A) Heat Map → CVD → VWAP → enter trade
B) VWAP position → CVD direction → Volume Profile context → Heat Map for precise entry
C) Look for a chart pattern, then check CVD, then enter immediately
D) Check news events first, then look at VWAP
✅ The correct flow is: VWAP gives daily bias → CVD confirms real-time aggression direction → Volume Profile gives structural context → Heat Map provides precise entry timing. Each layer narrows and confirms the trade.
