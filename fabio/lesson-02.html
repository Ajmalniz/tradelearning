Market Participants:
Who Moves the Price?

Meet the two forces behind every trade — and understand the bid/ask spread that sits between them.

⏱ 12 min 🟢 Beginner 📖 Foundation

The Two Types of Market Participants

Every transaction in a financial market involves exactly two sides. Understanding these two roles is crucial — because they have completely different characteristics, goals, and behavior:

Market Makers
The Passive Side · They Offer
Place limit orders at specific prices
Wait for price to come to them
Provide the liquidity that others consume
Have price certainty — they know the price they'll get
No execution certainty — they may never get filled
Collect the spread as compensation
Market Takers
The Aggressive Side · They Eat
Place market orders — execute immediately
Demand to be filled RIGHT NOW
Consume (remove) passive liquidity
Execution certainty — they will be filled
No price certainty — they pay whatever price is available
Pay the spread as the cost of urgency
"Market takers — they EAT. These are the bubbles that eat liquidity levels. Market makers — they OFFER." — Fabio

The Bid, the Ask, and the Spread

At any moment, the market has two prices: the best bid (highest price a buyer will pay) and the best ask (lowest price a seller will accept). The gap between them is the spread.

In this example, if you want to buy right now as a market taker, you pay 101 (the ask). If you want to sell right now, you receive 99 (the bid). The spread is the cost of immediacy — and the profit of providing liquidity.

The Crucial Insight About the Spread

Fabio explains this clearly: as a passive order, you buy and sell at the price you want — but you don't have a guarantee you'll get filled. That's the perspective of the passive order: price certainty but not time certainty and not execution certainty.

Aggressive market participants say "fill me NOW." They buy and sell when they want, but they pay a worse price — they pay for the spread because they have the necessity to be filled.

💡

Why This Matters for Trading

When you see aggressive orders (bubbles) on the heat map, you know someone was willing to pay the spread — they wanted to be filled so badly they didn't wait. Large aggressive orders are a signal of strong conviction and intention. They're far more meaningful than a small limit order sitting quietly on the book.

Institutional Players vs Retail Traders

Institutional players (banks, hedge funds, prop firms) have a unique challenge: they have to fill massive order sizes. You can't just dump 10,000 contracts at once — you'd move the market against yourself and get terrible fills.

So they use sophisticated methods:

🏦

How Institutions Fill Large Orders

Iceberg orders: Only show 8 contracts at a time, but reload the level hundreds of times to fill 500+ contracts total.

Algorithmic execution: Split huge orders into many small ones spread across time and price levels.

Passive accumulation: Reload passive liquidity levels to get filled without alerting the market.

Distribution: Sell into buying pressure, reload, sell again — without ever moving price against themselves.

🎯

What This Means for You

When you see a liquidity level on the heat map being repeatedly reloaded — even as price absorbs it again and again — that is institutional activity. They are building a position. This is one of the most powerful signals you can use to align yourself with the "smart money."

⚡ Key Takeaways from Lesson 2

  • Market Makers are passive — they offer limit orders and wait. They have price certainty, not execution certainty.
  • Market Takers are aggressive — they use market orders and demand fills immediately. They pay the spread.
  • The bid-ask spread is the cost of getting filled immediately vs. waiting for your price.
  • Aggressive orders (bubbles) are the force that physically moves price through levels.
  • Passive orders (resting liquidity) either stop price or support it through a level.
  • Institutions disguise large order flows using iceberg orders, algos, and passive reloading.

B) Someone who places limit orders that wait to be filled, providing passive liquidity
C) A hedge fund that always trades against retail traders
D) An algorithmic trading bot
✅ Market Makers provide passive liquidity with limit orders. They offer price certainty but have no guarantee of execution.

2. If the best bid is 99 and the best ask is 101, what is the spread?

A) 1 tick
B) 2 ticks
C) 100 ticks
D) 0 — they are the same price
✅ Spread = ask - bid = 101 - 99 = 2 ticks. As a market taker buying, you'd pay 101. Selling, you'd receive 99.

3. Why do market takers pay the spread?

A) They are unintelligent traders who don't understand the market
B) The exchange charges them an extra fee
C) They demand immediate execution and must take whatever price is available, crossing the spread
D) They always trade large sizes
✅ Market takers pay the spread because they prioritize execution speed over price. The cost of certainty is crossing the bid-ask spread.

4. Why do institutions use iceberg orders and algorithmic execution?

A) Because it's required by law
B) Because placing a massive single order would move the market against them, causing terrible fill prices
C) To manipulate markets illegally
D) Because they can't afford the spread on large orders
✅ Institutions split large orders to avoid market impact. A single 10,000-contract order would drive price dramatically, giving them terrible average fill prices.
